# Frame 12 - Desert Halt

## Speaker
Eugene

## Director Notes
Voice actor: Eugene / Lord E. Compton.
Performance: English aristocrat, educated officer, deep baritone or low resonant male voice. Controlled diction, precise consonants, old money without parody. He does not mumble. He speaks like a man trained to command under fire.
Pacing: measured, deliberate, never rushed. Shorter violent sentences may land harder and lower.
Emotional rule: restraint first; emotion leaks through cracks, not theatrical sobbing.

Scene emotion: dry field report, heat exhaustion, watchfulness, old dread in the air

## Voiceover Text
A second-hand half-track, purchased in Cairo for an indecent sum, is a monstrous contraption: part motorcar, part agricultural machine, part act of personal defiance against the desert. It is also the only vehicle we could acquire at such speed that will manage the wadis without a road.

No hired party accompanies us. There was no time to find men I could trust, and I shall not deliver the secret of my son's prison into the hands of strangers merely because they know the local tracks. We took advice in Cairo, bought what we required, loaded the machine ourselves, and left before any sensible person could ask where two English aristocrats intended to vanish before dawn.

We stop only long enough to check the engine, refill the radiator, and drink water that tastes of hot metal. Alice sits upon the bonnet with the map board across her knees, her hair tied back beneath a scarf, looking for all the world as though a halt in the Egyptian desert were a rather inconvenient garden party. Grandfather's coordinates are copied onto a separate sheet, clipped to her board. She cross-references them against a British military survey from 1916 -- the best we have for this district. The lines agree. The tomb is where Grandfather said it would be.

Dust. Heat. Silence. I stand on the running board and scan the horizon through field glasses -- sand, rock, sky, nothing else. And yet I feel... I do not know how to describe it. When we entered the Valley, something changed. As though the air grew thicker. As though something -- very large, very old -- turned its head in our direction.

Alice feels it too. I can see it in her face. Mbambwe taught her to hear what others cannot. She says nothing. She has gone pale.

We are going for our boy. Whatever awaits us.
