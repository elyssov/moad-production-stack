# Frame 09A - The Library Before Dawn, Eugene

## Speaker
Eugene

## Director Notes
Voice actor: Eugene / Lord E. Compton.
Performance: English aristocrat, educated officer, deep baritone or low resonant male voice. Controlled diction, precise consonants, old money without parody. He does not mumble. He speaks like a man trained to command under fire.
Pacing: measured, deliberate, never rushed. Shorter violent sentences may land harder and lower.
Emotional rule: restraint first; emotion leaks through cracks, not theatrical sobbing.

Scene emotion: sleep-deprived analysis, bitter family irony, breakthrough

## Voiceover Text
Grandfather's journals. Seven volumes, three crates of field notebooks, loose survey sheets, letters, receipts, sketches, lists of hired men, lists of dead men, and the private vanity of every Victorian explorer: the belief that nothing in the world exists until an Englishman has written it down.

For once, I am grateful for the vanity.

The difficulty is that the map names have shifted under our feet. "The Valley of the Kings" is plain enough when printed on a museum label; it is much less plain in Grandfather's oldest notebooks, where a day may be recorded as "the Western Wadis," "the southern necropolis," "the dry river beyond the known royal tombs," or simply "the place the men refused after sunset." A dozen names for one stretch of stone and sand, some formal, some local, several of them contradicting one another at precisely the moment we need precision.

Alice works beside me. She has wrapped a dressing gown around her nightdress at last, though she has not noticed the belt is tied crooked. She makes coffee strong enough to raise the dead, translates Arabic marginalia where my eyes begin to fail, and marks every possible location on the military survey. She does not ask whether I have found it. She knows: if I find it, I shall say. If I do not, I shall say that too. We have never learnt to be silent with one another, but we have learnt to be silent beside one another.

At twenty minutes past four, Volume Three gives us the first wound in the wall.

Grandfather's hand -- small, precise, with the characteristic flourishes on the capital letters that he carried from Eton to his dying day:

*"Mirror stone, extracted from an unnumbered tomb at the southern extremity of the dry valley beyond the royal burials. Surface of inexplicable blackness. Not obsidian. Not basalt. Sampling proved impossible -- every instrument slides without marking. The workmen refused to carry it. Hassan, the foreman, informed me that his men call the stone 'ba-ka-er' -- 'soul without a mouth.' I have found this term in no known lexicon. The stone was conveyed to camp by sledge, at night, without the presence of the local men. Coordinates of the tomb: ..."*

Coordinates. Not perfect -- nothing from Grandfather's first survey ever is -- but enough. A chain of bearings, a note about a broken ridge, a sketch of a cliff face, and a remark that at the time meant nothing:

*"Entrance made not by instrument. Rather by intention."*

Alice stands beside me. She looks at the page. Then at me. Then at the page. We both understand that we have not found a theory.

We have found an address.

Not the Stone's explanation. Not its mercy. Not our son.

But the place from which the door was torn.
