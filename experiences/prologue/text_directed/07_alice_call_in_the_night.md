# Frame 07 - The Call in the Night

## Speaker
Alice

## Director Notes
Voice actor: Alice / Lady A. Compton.
Performance: English aristocrat with warmth, intelligence, and private diary intimacy. Clear cultivated speech, elegant but alive. She is not fragile; she is tender and dangerous when pushed.
Pacing: slightly more fluid than Eugene, lyrical but not slow. Let the thought breathe.
Emotional rule: allow visible feeling, but keep the discipline of a woman raised to control herself in public.

Scene emotion: night terror, maternal panic, Lovecraftian dread, grief beginning

## Voiceover Text
It happened at night.

That is the fact I must write first, because every other fact feels less real than that one. Not in the garden. Not in daylight. Not while servants moved through the house and Eugene worked in the library and I could pretend that the world remained ruled by clocks, bells, meals, and reasonable English habits.

Night. The whole house asleep.

At first I thought I had dreamt it: a sound without sound, a pressure behind the eyes, a whisper laid directly upon the nerves. Not words. Worse than words. A summons. A hook passed through the soul and drawn tight by a hand that had never been human.

There are kinds of fear the body knows before the mind has found a name for them. This was older than fear. It was the knowledge that something vast had shifted in the dark, not toward me, not yet, but toward my child. The house seemed to breathe around it. The walls, the floors, the portraits of dead Comptons -- all of them listening.

I did not wake at once. God forgive me. Some small part of me woke before the rest -- the part that Mbambwe had awakened in Africa, the tiny splinter of her power that entered me when I dragged Eugene back from death. It heard the Call. It knew it. It tried to rise while the rest of me lay warm beside my husband, wrapped in sleep, and for several dreadful minutes my son rose from his bed and walked through the dark.

Then I woke screaming.

Not a clean scream. Not the elegant cry of a frightened lady in a novel. A raw animal sound, torn up from somewhere below speech.

Eugene was on his feet before I had even finished saying Alexander's name. He did not ask how I knew. He saw my face and reached for his dressing gown, his torch, anything his hands could find. I remember seizing his wrist. I remember saying, "We are too late," though I did not know for what. I remember his expression when he looked into my eyes, and only later did he tell me that they were shining faintly blue.

I dragged him through the corridors of Compton Hall by instinct alone -- barefoot, half-mad, following a pull inside my bones.

Down the stairs. Across the long gallery. Past portraits of dead Comptons staring down as though they knew and would not speak. Into the Egyptian Gallery.

And there he was.

Alexander stood before the Black Stone in his nightshirt. The surface was no longer stone. It was a vertical pool of absolute, matte blackness -- not shining, not glowing, not a mirror in any honest sense, but a dead depth in the shape of a mirror. It held him as water holds a body. One arm had already vanished. One shoulder. His chest.

And I swear before God -- I swear it knowing how mad it sounds -- there was something around his neck. Not rope. Not cloth. Something pale and slick and jointless, visible only where the torch caught it for the smallest fraction of a second. It tightened, not cruelly, not in anger, but with the dreadful patience of a thing drawing in a fish on a line.

He turned his head when we entered. I shall remember his face until the last second of my life. Not pain. Not even terror. Enchantment. Confusion. A little boy waking from one dream into another and not understanding why his mother was screaming.

Eugene ran.

So did I.

We were too late.

Alexander tried to reach for us. Or perhaps I only need to believe that he did. His fingers slipped across the edge of the black surface as though across wet glass. Then the Stone took him completely, and the darkness folded shut behind him.

No flash. No thunder. No mercy.

Only the Black Stone standing in its corner once more: rough on the outside, impossible on the face, matte and silent as a sealed tomb.

If the surface had remained open for one heartbeat longer, Eugene and I would have gone through it after him. Nightdress, dressing gown, bare feet, no weapons, no plan, no thought at all. We would have dived like fish into hell.

But the door closed.

And our son was on the other side.
