# Frame 05A - Alice Tends Wounded Eugene

## Speaker
Alice

## Director Notes
Voice actor: Alice / Lady A. Compton.
Performance: English aristocrat with warmth, intelligence, and private diary intimacy. Clear cultivated speech, elegant but alive. She is not fragile; she is tender and dangerous when pushed.
Pacing: slightly more fluid than Eugene, lyrical but not slow. Let the thought breathe.
Emotional rule: allow visible feeling, but keep the discipline of a woman raised to control herself in public.

Scene emotion: terrified certainty, first true magic, love arriving as fact

## Voiceover Text
...I saw him at triage. The wounds were dreadful -- shrapnel had opened him from shoulder to hip, and what the shrapnel had not done, the mud and the gas had finished. Three surgeons passed his stretcher. Each one glanced, shook his head, and moved to the next man.

I did not.

I do not know what it was. I have asked myself a thousand times since. Some instinct -- not medical, not rational, something older and deeper, something Mbambwe would have recognised and nodded at -- told me this man would live. Not *should* live. *Would.* As certainty. As fact. As something I knew the way one knows that water is wet and fire is hot.

I pulled his stretcher to the surgical tent myself. The duty surgeon -- a decent old fellow called Hargreaves, who had been cutting for thirty-six hours without sleep -- told me I was wasting theatre time on a corpse and sent me away. I came back. He told me I was a foolish girl who had fallen in love with a dead man. Perhaps he was right about the first part.

I remembered Mbambwe's teaching. I had never used it -- not truly, not in earnest, not with intent. But I placed my hands on his chest and I did what she had taught me: I listened for the thread. And I heard it -- faint, fraying, but there. And I pulled.

My hands began to glow. I was terrified. But I did not stop. And then -- he shuddered, and for a moment, a faint blue light passed through him, and his breathing steadied, and his colour changed, and I *knew.*

Ten minutes later, Surgeon-Major Hargreaves made the acquaintance of the full and formidable wrath of the daughter of one of the Empire's most senior lords. Five minutes after that, cursing himself for an old fool and begging my pardon with every stitch, he was closing wounds that he had been quite certain, not twenty minutes prior, were beyond all surgery.

I forgave the old man long ago. And in my heart, I thanked Mbambwe.

For the fact that he survived. And for the certainty -- which I felt as solidly as the floor beneath my feet -- that he would be mine.
