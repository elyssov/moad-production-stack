# Frame 04 - Alice, WWI Triage

## Speaker
Alice

## Director Notes
Voice actor: Alice / Lady A. Compton.
Performance: English aristocrat with warmth, intelligence, and private diary intimacy. Clear cultivated speech, elegant but alive. She is not fragile; she is tender and dangerous when pushed.
Pacing: slightly more fluid than Eugene, lyrical but not slow. Let the thought breathe.
Emotional rule: allow visible feeling, but keep the discipline of a woman raised to control herself in public.

Scene emotion: exhaustion, moral injury, compassion held together by discipline

## Voiceover Text
Good Lord... I came down to this hell of my own volition. I made this choice with my own two hands and my own stubborn, stupid, Cholmondeley pride. And now, every night, I weep into my pillow -- when I have the luxury of reaching a pillow at all.

The wounded come in a river that does not stop. Stretcher after stretcher after stretcher. And I -- a girl of twenty, with a VAD armband and six months of training -- I stand at the head of the line and play God. I decide who lives. I decide who... No. They will die regardless. But someone must decide who is denied even the hope of treatment, so that the ones who can be saved are not lost waiting behind those who cannot.

To-day, that someone was I. And yesterday. And to-morrow. And the day after, and the day after that, until this war ends or I do.

The soldiers call me "the girl who argues with God." They are not wrong. I argue. I insist. I drag men back from His doorstep by their collars and dare Him to object. Some of them live. Some of them die despite everything. The ones who die -- I remember their faces. Every single one.

The first one was barely twenty. Private Thomas Wynn, Welsh, blue-eyed, with shrapnel in places no boy should have to know he possesses. I sat with him until dawn because there was nothing else to do for him, and because no one should die reaching for a hand that is not there. He had a German Luger wrapped in a filthy puttee -- a souvenir, he said, taken fairly, and no good to him now. He pressed it into my palm and made me promise I would keep it.

*"Let it guard you, Lady Angel,"* he said.

That is how the nickname began. I told him not to be ridiculous. He smiled, squeezed my hand once, and died.

Since then the Luger has had an odd habit of being where I need it. I have used it on the front when the front came too close to the tents, and I have killed men with it. I write that sentence because truth must be written plainly, or it festers. I do not know whether Thomas would forgive me for what his pistol has done in my hand. I hope he would understand.

But as heavy as this burden is -- and it is unspeakably, indescribably heavy -- I shall rise to-morrow and force myself through triage once more. Because I do not only condemn. I save those who truly can be saved. And that -- that alone -- is enough to make me stand.
