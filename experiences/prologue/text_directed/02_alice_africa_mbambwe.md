# Frame 03 - Young Alice and Mbambwe

## Speaker
Alice

## Director Notes
Voice actor: Alice / Lady A. Compton.
Performance: English aristocrat with warmth, intelligence, and private diary intimacy. Clear cultivated speech, elegant but alive. She is not fragile; she is tender and dangerous when pushed.
Pacing: slightly more fluid than Eugene, lyrical but not slow. Let the thought breathe.
Emotional rule: allow visible feeling, but keep the discipline of a woman raised to control herself in public.

Scene emotion: wonder, youth, warmth, and a slow shadow of forbidden knowledge

## Voiceover Text
Some people believe Africa to be nothing more than savages, disease, and dangerous beasts. They are not entirely wrong, as far as it goes. But it goes nowhere near far enough. Africa is also the most beautiful country on God's earth, filled with people of extraordinary kindness and simplicity -- a simplicity which is not stupidity, whatever the colonial administrators may tell themselves over their gin.

Father's work was ethnography -- the study of peoples whom the rest of the Empire preferred not to think about. He took me everywhere. I was seventeen, burnt brown by the sun, and entirely certain I was immortal.

Mbambwe was the priestess of Nomkhubulwane -- the Great Mother of Rain, Flesh, and Ancestors. She took one look at me and said I had the hands of a woman who argues with death. I laughed. She did not.

While Father conducted his interviews and filled his notebooks, Mbambwe and I would sit by the fire, and she would teach me things that are not in any book. Herbs. Bones. The reading of breath. The speaking to that which has ceased to breathe.

I was seventeen, and I thought I was learning philosophy. I was not. I was learning a trade. And the trade had a price that Mbambwe knew and did not tell me.

Sometimes, at night, when the fire was low and the jungle pressed close, I felt certain that real magic existed. Not the parlour tricks of London mediums. The other kind. The kind that listens back.
