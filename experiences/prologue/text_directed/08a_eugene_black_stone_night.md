# Frame 08A - Parents at the Black Stone, Eugene

## Speaker
Eugene

## Director Notes
Voice actor: Eugene / Lord E. Compton.
Performance: English aristocrat, educated officer, deep baritone or low resonant male voice. Controlled diction, precise consonants, old money without parody. He does not mumble. He speaks like a man trained to command under fire.
Pacing: measured, deliberate, never rushed. Shorter violent sentences may land harder and lower.
Emotional rule: restraint first; emotion leaks through cracks, not theatrical sobbing.

Scene emotion: controlled terror, forensic rage, guilt, urgent resolve

## Voiceover Text
My wife woke me with murder in her eyes and a ghost-light in them.

That is not rhetoric. I have seen men wake under shellfire, seen officers rise from mud with half their faces gone and still try to give orders; I know the difference between panic and command. Alice was not panicking. She was already moving. Her hand closed on my wrist with such force that the next morning I found bruises in the shape of her fingers.

"Alexander," she said.

Then, before I could ask what had happened:

"We are too late."

Her eyes were blue. Not the colour of her eyes -- I know the colour of my wife's eyes better than I know the map of my own hands -- but lit from behind, faintly, impossibly, like winter lightning seen through cloud. She pulled me out of bed, thrust my dressing gown at me, seized the electric torch from the table, and dragged me through my own house as if she were following a voice that only she could hear.

I asked her once. She did not answer. She was whispering something under her breath. I caught only fragments:

"Not him. Not my boy. We are not in time. Eugene, run."

So I ran.

And twenty yards away, across the Egyptian Gallery, I saw my son in the Stone.

Two o'clock in the morning. I stand in the museum gallery in my dressing gown, an electric torch in one hand and a magnifying glass in the other, examining the object that devoured my son. Alice is beside me. She has not changed out of her nightdress. Neither of us has had the wit, decency, or strength to pretend that clothing matters.

The Stone gives us nothing.

Fifty minutes ago, I saw my boy vanish into it. I saw the surface behave as no surface on God's earth has any right to behave. I saw my son reach back toward us while the blackness swallowed him to the throat.

And I saw something else.

God help me, I saw it. A pale coil, or tendril, or rope of living cartilage -- I cannot name it without making the thing smaller than it was -- wound once around his neck and shoulder. It did not thrash. It did not strike. It simply drew him inward with the calm inevitability of a tide. By the time my foot touched the gallery floor, Alexander was gone.

And now the damned thing stands there as though it were merely an exhibit badly catalogued by a lazy ancestor.

Smooth. Black. Cold.

Its surface temperature is below that of the room, although the room is warm and has been warm all day. Grandfather noted the same anomaly in his journal. He attached no significance to it. I attached no significance to it. Three generations of Comptons -- and not one of us troubled to ask the question: why is this stone colder than it ought to beE

Because we were occupied with more interesting things. Papyri. Frescoes. Royal necropolises. An unclassified stone was not a mystery; it was baggage. We walked past. All of us walked past. And my boy paid for our indifference.

Alice has placed both hands upon the face three times. Nothing. I have tested the edges, the back, the floor beneath it, the frame of rough rock around the black plane. Nothing. No seam. No latch. No depression. No measurable vibration. No inscription visible under electric light, candle flame, or magnifying lens.

There is only the shape of a door after the door has ceased to be a door.

Alice wishes to strike it. Then to kiss it. Then to cut her palm and press blood to it, because grief makes barbarians of us all, and because somewhere in her blood something answered that Call before she woke. I hold her by the wrists until she stops shaking. Not because I am braver -- good God, what courage; I am frightened to the point of nausea -- but because if the Stone opens for her and takes her as well, then Alexander will be orphaned on both sides of this accursed wall.

That I should not survive. Physically -- perhaps. In every other sense -- no.

Therefore we shall not throw ourselves against a closed door. We shall find where the other side is anchored. This thing has a provenance. Grandfather brought it from Egypt. Not from "the Valley of the Kings" in the clean, modern, catalogue sense that makes curators sound more certain than they are, but from one of the southern wadis beyond the comfortably numbered tombs -- described in his earliest notes by bearings, workmen's warnings, bad air, and an entrance no registry appears to contain.

He wrote everything down.

And I have his library.
