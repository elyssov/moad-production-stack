## Presentation Rule

Each story beat should play as:

1. Show the still image first, clean and readable.
2. Use a slow cinematic move only if it preserves the art.
3. Fade or cut into the relevant writing surface.
4. Alice text appears on the Moleskine page as private handwriting.
5. Eugene text appears on the Underwood/typewriter setup as hard-struck text.
6. When both speak, alternate their writing surfaces unless a final shared line
   is explicitly marked as together.

Do not use a generic subtitle box over the art as the primary presentation.
The notebook and typewriter are part of the storytelling.

## Voice Direction Summary

Alice and Eugene are cultivated English aristocrats, not modern casual
narrators. They should sound educated, precise, and emotionally disciplined.

Alice:

- Adult female English aristocrat, warm, intelligent, privately intimate.
- Her narration is diary-like: elegant, alive, not fragile.
- She can show feeling more openly than Eugene, but she still has public-school
  discipline and old-family composure.
- Under fear or grief her voice may crack, but it must not become melodrama.
- She is tender and dangerous when pushed.

Eugene:

- Adult male English aristocrat, deep baritone or low resonant voice.
- Educated officer, commander under fire, precise consonants.
- Old money without parody; no cartoon lord, no generic fantasy king.
- He is not a heavy regional Scots accent just because he served in the Royal
  Scots Fusiliers.
- Emotion is compressed. Grief and love leak through cracks in restraint.
- In war passages he is controlled, hard, and frighteningly clear.

Together:

- A married couple who have survived war, grief, and terror.
- Intimate, resolved, and dangerous.
- Final lines should be calm enough to be believed, not shouted.
